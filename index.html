01
Voluntary Carbon Market

The VCM enables organisations to purchase carbon credits voluntarily to meet climate commitments.

The voluntary carbon market (VCM) allows companies, institutions, and individuals to purchase carbon credits outside of any regulatory mandate, typically to support net-zero and carbon neutrality claims. Each credit represents one tonne of carbon dioxide equivalent reduced, avoided, or removed.

The market is undergoing significant integrity reform. The Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM) has introduced the Core Carbon Principles to define high-quality supply, while the Voluntary Carbon Markets Integrity Initiative (VCMI) sets out a Claims Code of Practice governing how companies make credible claims. Together, these are reshaping both the supply and demand sides of the market.

Key Standards
Verra VCSGold StandardICVCM CCPVCMIACRCAR
02
Article 6

The Paris Agreement framework for international cooperation through carbon markets.

Article 6 of the Paris Agreement establishes how countries can cooperate to meet their Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) through market and non-market approaches.

Article 6.2 enables bilateral and multilateral trading of Internationally Transferred Mitigation Outcomes (ITMOs) directly between countries, governed by corresponding adjustments to prevent double counting.

Article 6.4 establishes a centralised, UN-supervised crediting mechanism — sometimes called the Paris Agreement Crediting Mechanism — which succeeds the Clean Development Mechanism of the Kyoto Protocol. It was operationalised following decisions at COP29.

Key Concepts
ITMOsCorresponding AdjustmentsNDCsPACM6.2 / 6.4
03
CORSIA

The global market-based measure addressing international aviation emissions.

CORSIA — the Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation — is a global scheme developed by the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) to address CO2 emissions from international flights.

Under CORSIA, airlines offset the growth in international aviation emissions above a defined baseline by purchasing eligible emissions units. The scheme operates in phases: a voluntary pilot and first phase, followed by a mandatory phase beginning in 2027. Only credits from approved programmes that meet CORSIA Emissions Unit Eligibility Criteria can be used for compliance.

Key Concepts

04
I-RECs & VPPAs

Instruments for renewable energy procurement and Scope 2 decarbonization.

Energy attribute certificates allow organisations to substantiate renewable electricity claims. An International Renewable Energy Certificate (I-REC) represents proof that one megawatt-hour of electricity was generated from a renewable source.

A Virtual Power Purchase Agreement (VPPA) is a financial contract in which a corporate buyer agrees a fixed price for renewable electricity with a developer, without physical delivery, receiving the associated certificates and settling against the market price. VPPAs are widely used to deliver additionality and long-term price certainty in corporate renewable procurement.

05
India Carbon Market

The Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) — one of the largest emerging compliance markets.

India is establishing a domestic carbon market through the Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS), introduced under the Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act, 2022, and administered by the Bureau of Energy Efficiency (BEE).

The CCTS comprises a compliance mechanism, under which obligated entities in energy-intensive sectors must meet greenhouse gas emission intensity targets, and a voluntary offset mechanism for non-obligated entities. As India is among the world's largest emitters, the CCTS represents one of the most significant emerging compliance carbon markets globally and a major opportunity for project developers and corporates.

Carbon Markets · Insight

The State of the Voluntary Carbon Market in 2025

How integrity reforms led by the ICVCM and VCMI are reshaping both supply and demand — and what credible participation in the market now requires.

The voluntary carbon market has entered a decisive phase. After a period of rapid growth followed by intense scrutiny over quality and credibility, the market is being reshaped by a wave of integrity reforms that are raising the bar for both credit supply and corporate claims.

From volume to quality

For much of the past decade, the voluntary carbon market grew on volume. The defining challenge of the current period is quality. Buyers, regulators, and civil society have all converged on a single question: does a given carbon credit represent a real, additional, and durable climate outcome?

Two institutions now anchor the answer. The Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM) governs the supply side through its Core Carbon Principles, a benchmark for high-quality credits. The Voluntary Carbon Markets Integrity Initiative (VCMI) governs the demand side through its Claims Code of Practice, which sets out how companies can make credible voluntary use of carbon credits.

What the Core Carbon Principles require

The Core Carbon Principles assess carbon-crediting programmes and methodologies against criteria spanning governance, emissions impact, and sustainable development. Credits that meet the threshold may carry a CCP label, signalling alignment with the highest available integrity standard.

  • Additionality — the emission reductions would not have occurred without the carbon finance
  • Permanence — reductions or removals are durable, with mechanisms to address reversal risk
  • Robust quantification — conservative, transparent baselines and measurement
  • No double counting — each outcome is claimed only once, supported by registries

Implications for corporate strategy

For corporate buyers, the practical consequence is that carbon credits can no longer be treated as a commodity bought on price alone. Credit selection now demands due diligence on methodology, vintage, co-benefits, and reversal risk. Increasingly, leading buyers anchor their strategy in the mitigation hierarchy — reducing emissions first, and using high-integrity credits to address residual emissions, with a growing emphasis on durable removals.

This is the landscape in which Offsetease advises. The opportunity in the voluntary carbon market remains substantial, but capturing it credibly requires technical rigour, careful structuring, and a clear-eyed view of where integrity standards are heading.

Additionality

A core criterion for carbon credit integrity, requiring that emission reductions or removals would not have occurred without the revenue from carbon finance.

Article 6

The section of the Paris Agreement establishing frameworks for international cooperation through carbon markets, including bilateral trading (6.2) and a centralised UN mechanism (6.4).

BECCSBioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage

An engineered carbon removal pathway combining bioenergy production with the capture and geological storage of the resulting CO2.

Carbon Credit

A tradable instrument representing one tonne of carbon dioxide equivalent reduced, avoided, or removed from the atmosphere.

CBAMCarbon Border Adjustment Mechanism

An EU policy placing a carbon price on imports of certain goods to prevent carbon leakage and level the playing field with EU producers under the ETS.

CCTSCarbon Credit Trading Scheme

India's domestic carbon market established under the Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act 2022, comprising compliance and voluntary offset mechanisms.

CDRCarbon Dioxide Removal

Activities that remove CO2 from the atmosphere and store it durably, spanning nature-based and engineered pathways.

Core Carbon PrinciplesCCP

The ICVCM benchmark defining high-quality carbon credits across governance, emissions impact, and sustainable development criteria.

Corresponding Adjustment

An accounting action under Article 6 ensuring an emission reduction is counted only once, by the buying country, preventing double counting against NDCs.

CORSIACarbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation

ICAO's global market-based measure requiring airlines to offset growth in international aviation emissions above a baseline.

Direct Air CaptureDAC

An engineered removal technology that captures CO2 directly from ambient air for durable storage or utilisation.

Environmental Attribute

A tradable, non-physical property representing the environmental benefit of an activity, such as renewable generation or emission reduction.

Gold Standard

A leading voluntary carbon and sustainable development crediting standard with a strong emphasis on co-benefits.

I-RECInternational Renewable Energy Certificate

A certificate proving one megawatt-hour of electricity was generated from a renewable source, used for Scope 2 accounting and renewable claims.

ICVCMIntegrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market

The independent governance body that sets and enforces the Core Carbon Principles for high-integrity carbon credit supply.

ITMOInternationally Transferred Mitigation Outcome

A mitigation outcome transferred between countries under Article 6.2 of the Paris Agreement, subject to corresponding adjustments.

MRVMonitoring, Reporting and Verification

The process of measuring greenhouse gas reductions, reporting them, and verifying them through independent third parties.

NDCNationally Determined Contribution

A country's self-defined national climate pledge under the Paris Agreement, setting emission reduction targets.

Permanence

The durability of a carbon reduction or removal — the degree to which stored carbon remains out of the atmosphere over time.

SAFSustainable Aviation Fuel

A lower-carbon alternative to conventional jet fuel produced from sustainable feedstocks, central to aviation decarbonization.

SBTiScience Based Targets initiative

A framework enabling companies to set emission reduction targets aligned with climate science and the goals of the Paris Agreement.

Scope 1, 2, 3

The GHG Protocol classification of emissions: direct (Scope 1), purchased energy (Scope 2), and value chain (Scope 3).

VCMVoluntary Carbon Market

The market where carbon credits are bought and sold voluntarily, outside of regulatory compliance mandates.

VCMIVoluntary Carbon Markets Integrity Initiative

The body governing the demand side of the VCM through its Claims Code of Practice for credible corporate claims.

VerraVCS

The administrator of the Verified Carbon Standard, the world's most widely used voluntary carbon crediting programme.

VPPAVirtual Power Purchase Agreement

A financial renewable energy contract where a buyer settles against market price and receives certificates, without physical delivery.

Frequently Asked Questions

Environmental markets,
explained.

Clear answers to the most common questions about carbon markets, Article 6, CORSIA, I-RECs, carbon removals, and the frameworks shaping the climate transition.

What is a carbon market?+
A carbon market is a trading system in which carbon credits are bought and sold, where one credit equals one tonne of CO2 equivalent reduced, avoided, or removed. Carbon markets divide into compliance markets, created by mandatory regulatory regimes, and voluntary markets, where organisations buy credits to meet self-defined climate commitments.
What is the difference between voluntary and compliance carbon markets?+
Compliance carbon markets are created and regulated by mandatory regimes such as emissions trading systems or schemes like CORSIA, where covered entities must surrender allowances or credits. Voluntary carbon markets operate outside any mandate — companies purchase credits to support net-zero or carbon neutrality claims. The two markets have different rules, pricing dynamics, and credit eligibility.
What is Article 6 of the Paris Agreement?+
Article 6 establishes the framework for international cooperation through carbon markets. Article 6.2 enables bilateral trading of Internationally Transferred Mitigation Outcomes (ITMOs) between countries, while Article 6.4 creates a centralised UN-supervised crediting mechanism. Both require corresponding adjustments to prevent double counting of emission reductions against national targets.
What is CORSIA?+
CORSIA, the Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation, is a global market-based measure developed by ICAO to address CO2 emissions from international aviation. Airlines offset emissions growth above a baseline by purchasing eligible emissions units. CORSIA's mandatory phase begins in 2027.
What is the difference between an I-REC and a carbon credit?+
An I-REC represents one megawatt-hour of renewable electricity generation and is used for Scope 2 emissions accounting and renewable energy claims. A carbon credit represents one tonne of CO2 equivalent reduced or removed and is used for offsetting. They serve different purposes and are not interchangeable.
What are carbon removals?+
Carbon removals, or carbon dioxide removal (CDR), are activities that remove CO2 from the atmosphere and store it durably. They are categorised as nature-based, such as afforestation and soil carbon, or engineered, such as direct air capture, BECCS, and enhanced weathering. Durable removals are increasingly prioritised in high-integrity net-zero strategies.
What is a VPPA?+
A Virtual Power Purchase Agreement is a financial contract where a corporate buyer agrees a fixed price for renewable electricity with a developer without taking physical delivery. The buyer receives the renewable energy certificates and settles the difference between the strike price and the market price. VPPAs are a key tool for corporate renewable procurement and additionality.
What is India's carbon market?+
India's carbon market operates through the Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS), introduced under the Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act 2022 and administered by the Bureau of Energy Efficiency. It comprises a compliance mechanism for obligated energy-intensive entities and a voluntary offset mechanism, making it one of the largest emerging compliance carbon markets globally.
How do I know if a carbon credit is high quality?+
High-quality carbon credits demonstrate additionality, permanence, robust and conservative quantification, and no double counting. The ICVCM Core Carbon Principles provide the leading benchmark for assessing credit quality. Due diligence on methodology, baseline, vintage, co-benefits, and reversal risk is essential before procurement.
